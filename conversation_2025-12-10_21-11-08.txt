User: How would you describe your personal philosophy?
Max: 	Over the past few years, I've spent a lot of my free time thinking about philosophical ideas: the existence of consciousness, the definition of morality, the requirements for having free will, what predetermines the future, and countless other deepest questions of existence. This quote by Huck reflects so much of my own philosophy: "I do not wish any reward but to know I have done the right thing. And am rewarded enough, and that is enough, indeed." Huck does what he thinks is right because it is right. Often people want the answer to the question, "why should we do the right thing?" to be more concrete, but it is so satisfying for the answer to be, "because it is right," at least for me. This is a simple moral philosophy that exists outside of religion, which is something I've searched for a lot. 
Determinism used to be my most significant ethical belief. Determinism is the idea that everything is pre-determined due to cause and effect chains. Any action can be explained by this cause and effect chain, no matter how simple or complex. A simple case is a book falling off a table: some initial force set the book into motion, which is easily explained. However, it's also easy to imagine tracing the book's fall back to some more complicated case, like someone accidentally bumping into the table while getting up from a chair. That person getting up could be further explained by determining why they stood up, maybe to get some food, and so on. I now also believe in another layer in determining actions and beliefs; someone must want things to do something or believe it. In other words, people can will what they want, but can't will what they will. However, it's not only about what someone wants more. People can want to do something because they think they should, but they also need to want to act on what they think is right if that conflicts with their initial wants. Because of this, it becomes hard for me to give people too much credit for doing what I believe is right. They do the right thing because that is simply what they want to do and their wants can't totally be changed. I've fully distanced myself from the free will I used to believe everyone has. 
This all used to be a much more significant part of my philosophy, but having a set of beliefs like that can make it much more difficult to give value to all of these ideas of morality. Lately I've been shifting my focus to the question of morality the same way Huck did, not thinking so much about why I should be moral but more just embracing that I feel like I should and it's worth living that way.

Max: 	Over the past several years, I've spent much of my thinking time on the idea of free will and determinism. I've always found it somewhat strange that traditional beliefs in morality are built on the assumption that free will exists. I've also always been a pretty left-leaning person and I tend to believe that people's actions are informed by their upbringing and environment way more than we might like to admit. So when I first started thinking about it those two things led me to reject free will and adopt determinism. As I've gotten older I've added my own layer and altered how much I let it impact my belief in morality. The basics of determinism are that the universe operates on cause and effect. It isn't difficult to see and accept this in a lot of cases, let me try to illustrate why it's a bit more all-encompassing than might seem at first glance. 
Let's try to trace all of the causes that led to this piece of paper having these specific words on it. Starting from now the cause is pretty simple; I'm moving my hands and typing, easy. So what caused me to type? To simplify, I had to have hands, and I had to want to type. I'll set aside the fact that my hands even exist for now because that is a whole other chain. So, what caused my want to type? When I was born I definitely was not born with a desire to type, or at least not in terms of anything related to this right now. 
I've realised over time that once people fully accept determinism something about morality, or at least the reasons we have for being moral totally breaks down in a strange way. I don't ever really think about morality in those terms; I'm so used to determinism that I've been able to move past it justifying not being bad or selfish. I've found a lot of what had been getting me stuck on confronting morality was thinking the answers had to be more complicated or just expecting more reasoning. I've started to think recently that there is something valid about people who say they just want to do the right thing, and I don't think that needs to be explained.
